Correlations Discovered Between Health Risks and Age, Poverty, and Income


Poverty vs. Lack of Healthcare

In general healthcare seems to be lacking when poverty levels are higher. However, there are exceptions: Texas and Alaska have the highest number of adults who lack healthcare despite having lower poverty rates.

Poverty vs. Smoking

When comparing the two factors, the percentage of smokers appear to be consistent across the the states, with a slight rise as poverty rates increase.

Poverty vs. Obesity

Obesity is generally high across all states, even states with low povert rates have high rates of obesity. Therefore there is a very weak correlation between the two factors.

Age vs. Lack of Healthcare

Adults between the age of 36-40 appear to have inadequate healthcare coverage. Texas has the highest percentage of the population who lacks healthcare.

Age vs. Smoking

It appeara that nationwide a large proportion of smokers are 36 years and older. Utah is the only state with a low median age and the lowest smoking rate.

Age vs. Obesity

Obesity appears to increse as the population ages. Thus, obesity is on the high in states where the median age is 36-40 years.

Income vs. Lack of Healthcare

Low income households are less likely to make adequate investments towards healthcare coverage.

Income vs. Smoking

Smoking seems to be more apparent among communities with lower incomes. Hence states with relatively high incomes have some of the lowest smoking rates.

Income vs. Obesity

Obesity is mostly high across all states. However, when compared to income, obesity levels seem to lessen with increase in household income. Similarly obesity levels are higher in states with lower income levels.
