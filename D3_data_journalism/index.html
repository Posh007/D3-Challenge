Correlations Discovered Between Health Risks and Age, Poverty, and Income

Poverty vs. Lack of Healthcare

In general, healthcare seems to be lacking when the levels of poverty are higher. However, there are exceptions: Texas and Alaska have the highest number of adults who lack healthcare despite having lower poverty rates.

Poverty vs. Smoking

When comparing the two factors, the percentage of smokers appear to be consistent across the the states, with a slight rise as poverty rates increase.

Poverty vs. Obesity

Obesity is generally high across all states, even states with low poverty rates have high rates of obesity. Therefore there seem to be a very weak correlation between these two factors.

Age vs. Lack of Healthcare

Adults between the age of 36-40 appear to have inadequate healthcare coverage. Texas appears to have the highest percentage of the population who lacks healthcare.

Age vs. Smoking

Analysis shows that nationwide a large proportion of smokers are 36 years and older. Utah is the only state with a low median age and the lowest smoking rate.

Age vs. Obesity

Obesity seems to increase as the population ages. Thus, obesity is higher in states where the median age is 36-40 years.

Household Income vs. Lack of Healthcare

States with a higher median household income appear to have better healthcare coverahe rates than those with lower incomes.

Household Income vs. Smoking

Smoking seems to be more apparent among states with large communities with lower incomes. Hence states with relatively high incomes have some of the lowest smoking rates.

Household Income vs. Obesity

Obesity is generally high across all states due to a variety of reasons. However, when compared to income, obesity levels seem to lessen with increase in household income.
